EMI Operational
Checklist

A practical, qualitative tool for applying EMI before, during, and after collaboration — designed to remain reflective rather than managerial.

01

How to use this page

EMI is used here as an operational, reflective tool. The point is not to score a collaboration, but to help artist-researchers and collaborators identify what artistic research is doing, where tensions emerge, and what still cannot be named clearly.

Before the project

Use EMI to clarify role, expectations, knowledge types, and likely sites of friction before they stay implicit.

During the project

Use EMI to document shifts, tensions, reframings, and mismatches between what the collaboration asks for and what it actually produces.

After the project

Use EMI to reflect on what changed, what remained unresolved, and where the framework itself failed to capture the work.

02

Operational checklist

Dimension
Guiding question
Possible indicators
Evidence traces
Present?
Notes / tensions
1. Problem Reconfiguration
Did the artistic work shift how the issue or research problem was framed?
New research questions; changed vocabulary; altered scope; movement from output to perceptual framing
Meeting notes; revised briefs; new formulations in emails or presentations
What changed? Who noticed? Was the shift welcomed or resisted?
2. Knowledge Integration
What kinds of knowledge were brought into relation?
Scientific + experiential; embodied + technical; local + formal; artistic + disciplinary
Workshop materials; diagrams; collaborative decisions; synthesis moments
Which knowledge types remained disconnected?
3. Epistemic Position & Authority
Did the artistic intervention affect who could define relevance, meaning, or direction?
Changes in who leads; redistribution of interpretive authority; tension around legitimacy
Meeting dynamics; authorship decisions; feedback patterns; role descriptions
Did the artist-researcher shape questions, or only outputs?
4. Experiential Reorientation
Did participants begin to sense, perceive, or relate to the issue differently?
Statements like "I see it differently now"; affective response; altered relation to data or models
Testimonials; observation notes; audience or collaborator reflections
Was the shift durable, superficial, or only momentary?
5. Evaluation Friction
Where did existing evaluative language fail to capture what the work was doing?
Pressure for measurable outcomes; mismatch with deliverables; disagreement over value
Consortium language; funding logic; debrief notes; conflict points
What could be felt but not adequately named?
6. Context & Stage Sensitivity
Under what institutional conditions and at what stage did the contribution become visible?
Differences between planning, implementation, reflection; timeline, governance, funding pressures
Project schedule; changes over time; stage-specific observations
Would the same contribution appear in another setting?

04

Short protocol for applying EMI

  1. Define the collaborative setting in one sentence.
  2. List the actual role, not the idealized role.
  3. Fill the EMI checklist quickly from memory.
  4. Locate evidence traces that support, nuance, or contradict the first pass.
  5. Mark where EMI helps and where it fails to capture the case.
  6. Use those failure points as inputs for EMI v0.4.
05

Important caution

EMI should not become a tool that flattens artistic research into checklist compliance. Its usefulness depends on remaining diagnostic, reflective, and open to revision. The failure points are as important as where it holds.
