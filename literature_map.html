Literature Map

A structured map of the framework traditions surrounding EMI: what already exists, what each tradition evaluates well, and where the specific gap addressed here begins.

Literature MapEMI ChecklistComparative Matrix
This page translates the two literature syntheses into a working map for the project. Its purpose is not to replace direct reading of primary sources, but to clarify which framework traditions already exist, what they help with, and where EMI can enter without overclaiming novelty.

1. Main deduction

The literature does not suggest that there is no evaluative language for artistic research or transdisciplinary collaboration. Instead, it shows two partially connected traditions:

Artistic research evaluation

  • quality, impact, contextuality
  • process reflexivity
  • transformation and non-propositional knowledge
  • practitioner-facing and qualitative tools

Transdisciplinary epistemic integration

  • problem framing
  • knowledge integration across knowledge types
  • authority / representation / power
  • context- and stage-sensitive assessment

EMI becomes plausible precisely at the intersection of these two traditions.

3. What this means for EMI

What EMI should not be

  • a universal metric
  • a KPI dashboard
  • a proof-machine for the usefulness of art
  • a replacement for case-sensitive judgement

What EMI can be

  • a practitioner-facing analytic vocabulary
  • a reflective tool usable before, during, and after collaboration
  • a bridge between artistic research evaluation and TD epistemic integration
  • a way to name frictions and blind spots

What remains open

  • Does EMI help in practice?
  • Which settings are best for testing it?
  • How much does it need co-design with other stakeholders?
  • Where does it fail or overreach?

4. Working gap statement

Existing frameworks can assess impact, quality, collaboration, and knowledge integration. What seems less developed is a vocabulary that helps artist-researchers articulate their epistemic contribution from within collaboration itself.

5. Immediate implications for the FORUM+ article

  • The article should not claim that “nothing exists.” It should claim that the existing traditions do not quite solve the practitioner-facing articulation problem.
  • The article should frame EMI as exploratory, qualitative, and context-sensitive.
  • The article can legitimately use HLaS and ATMOS_FERA as two probes that expose the need for something like EMI, even if they do not validate it.
  • The paper’s strongest niche is not impact reporting, but role articulation, epistemic contribution, and collaborative positioning.
