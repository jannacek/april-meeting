Literature Map

A structured map of the framework traditions surrounding EMI — what already exists, what each tradition evaluates well, and where the specific gap addressed here begins.

01

Main deduction

The literature does not suggest there is no evaluative language for artistic research or transdisciplinary collaboration. It shows two partially connected traditions — and EMI becomes plausible at their intersection.

Artistic research evaluation

  • quality, impact, contextuality
  • process reflexivity
  • transformation and non-propositional knowledge
  • practitioner-facing and qualitative tools

Transdisciplinary epistemic integration

  • problem framing
  • knowledge integration across knowledge types
  • authority / representation / power
  • context- and stage-sensitive assessment

03

What this means for EMI

What EMI should not be

  • a universal metric
  • a proof-machine for the usefulness of art
  • a replacement for case-sensitive judgement
  • a finished model

What EMI can be

  • a practitioner-facing analytic vocabulary
  • a reflective tool usable before, during, and after collaboration
  • a bridge between two framework traditions
  • a way to name frictions and blind spots

What remains open

  • Does EMI help in practice?
  • Which settings are best for testing it?
  • How much co-design does it require?
  • Where does it fail or overreach?
04

Working gap statement

Existing frameworks can assess impact, quality, collaboration, and knowledge integration. What seems less developed is a vocabulary that helps artist-researchers articulate their epistemic contribution from within collaboration itself — not after the fact, but as part of the process.

05

Implications for the FORUM+ article

  • The article should not claim "nothing exists." It should claim that the existing traditions do not solve the practitioner-facing articulation problem.
  • The article should frame EMI as exploratory, qualitative, and context-sensitive.
  • HLaS and ATMOS_FERA function as two probes that expose the need for something like EMI — not as validation.
  • The paper's niche: role articulation, epistemic contribution, and collaborative positioning — not impact reporting.
